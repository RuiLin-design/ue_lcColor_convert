Bypassing ACES Tonemapping:
Fixing Color Shifts in 3D UI Widgets

UI/UX Technical Artist
Published Apr 9, 2026

You've carefully converted your sRGB HEX codes to Linear Space, and your 2D screen-space menus look flawless. But the moment you attach that same UMG widget to an actor in the 3D world—like a VR wrist menu, a holographic floating screen, or a diegetic computer terminal—your brand colors suddenly look completely wrong. Pure blues turn into cyan, and bright reds shift into neon orange. Welcome to the ACES Tonemapping trap.

The Rendering Pipeline Divide

To solve this, we must first understand how Unreal Engine prioritizes rendering passes:

  • 2D Screen-Space UI (Viewport UMG): These widgets are rendered at the very end of the pipeline. They sit directly on top of the final rendered frame and completely bypass the scene's lighting, shadows, and post-processing. What you input is exactly what you get.
  • 3D World-Space UI (Widget Components): When a widget is placed in the 3D world, it is treated like any other static mesh. It gets rendered during the scene pass. Consequently, it is subjected to the camera's exposure, bloom, and crucially, the Post Process Volume's color grading and tonemapping.

The ACES Color Shift Explained

Unreal Engine 5 defaults to using the ACES (Academy Color Encoding System) filmic tonemapper. ACES is fantastic for achieving photorealistic, cinematic renders because it mimics how real film handles overexposure—it gently rolls off highlights and desaturates extremely bright colors.

However, ACES is famously hostile to highly saturated "digital" colors. A pure brand blue (R=0, G=0, B=1) pushed through ACES will skew towards magenta or cyan. A pure digital red will shift towards orange. For UI designers who require absolute brand color accuracy, this filmic shift is unacceptable.

The Ultimate Fix: Material Domain

You don't need to turn off ACES for your whole game (which would ruin your 3D graphics). Instead, you need to tell the engine to treat your specific 3D widget material differently. We do this by changing the Material Domain.

Step 1: Create a Custom Retainer Material

By default, Widget Components use a built-in material (like Widget3DPassThrough). To bypass lighting, you need to create your own material and apply it to the Widget Component.

  • Create a new Material in the Content Browser.
  • In the Details panel, change the Material Domain from Surface to User Interface.
  • Change the Blend Mode to Translucent (or Masked if you don't need partial transparency).

Step 2: Connecting the Nodes

Because the domain is now "User Interface", your main material node will look much smaller. It only accepts Final Color and Screen Position.

⚠️ The Secret Sauce: When a Material is set to the 'User Interface' domain, Unreal Engine mathematically guarantees that whatever color is fed into the 'Final Color' pin will bypass the ACES tonemapper and output directly to the monitor's display buffer.

Simply sample your Widget's Render Target (using a TextureSampleParameter2D) and plug the RGB into Final Color, and the Alpha into the Opacity pin.

Step 3: Assigning in the Widget Component

Finally, select your Widget Component in your Actor's blueprint hierarchy:

// In the Details Panel of your Widget Component:
1. Locate the 'Rendering' section.
2. Find the 'Material' override property.
3. Assign the Custom UI Material you just created.
4. Ensure 'Tint Color and Opacity' is set to purely White (1,1,1,1).

Summary: The Golden Rules of UE5 Colors

  • 2D Viewport UI: Always bypasses post-processing. Just ensure your HEX to Linear conversion is accurate.
  • 3D Widget (Surface Domain): Reacts to lights, casts shadows, and gets distorted by ACES Tonemapping. Good for grimy, diegetic screens (like a broken TV in a horror game).
  • 3D Widget (User Interface Domain): Unlit, ignores shadows, and completely bypasses ACES. Essential for crisp, brand-accurate VR menus and AR overlays.

With your material domain set correctly, your 3D widgets will now boast the exact same vivid, accurate colors as your 2D designs. Happy developing!
